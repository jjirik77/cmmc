CMMC Applicability: Clauses, Decisions, Scope & Flowdown

Learn how to read the contract clauses that trigger CMMC, decide whether and at what level it applies, define what gets assessed, and trace obligations down the supply chain — everything you need before the FFSI case study.

🎯 What This Lesson Covers

An applicability study answers one question before a contractor spends a dollar on compliance: “Does CMMC apply to us — and if so, why, where, and at what level?” This lesson teaches the four moving parts that answer it.

Before you start

You've already covered FCI vs. CUI and personnel applicability in earlier lessons. Here we focus on the other four pieces — the clauses, the decision logic, the scope/boundary, and flowdown — and use your FCI/CUI knowledge as an input.

By the end, you will be able to:

  1. Identify the FAR/DFARS clauses that trigger safeguarding and CMMC obligations, and state what each requires.
  2. Apply a decision tree to determine whether CMMC applies and at what level (1, 2, or 3).
  3. Define the CMMC assessment scope and sort assets into the five categories to set the assessment boundary.
  4. Explain how CMMC obligations flow down to subcontractors who handle covered information.

Module 1 The Clauses That Trigger CMMC

CMMC never appears out of nowhere — it is triggered by contract language. A handful of clauses in a federal contract tell you whether the work touches protected information and what you must do about it. Think of the clauses as the “ignition switch”: if they're in the contract, obligations turn on. Your first job in any applicability study is to read the contract and find them.

Two regulation families matter: FAR (the Federal Acquisition Regulation — applies to nearly all federal contracts) and DFARS (the Defense FAR Supplement — adds DoD-specific cybersecurity rules).

FAR 52.204-21
Basic Safeguarding of Covered Contractor Information Systems
Applies when a contractor handles FCI (Federal Contract Information). Requires 15 basic safeguarding requirements. This is the floor for almost every federal contract → maps to CMMC Level 1.
DFARS 252.204-7012
Safeguarding CDI & Cyber Incident Reporting
Applies when the contract involves CUI / Covered Defense Information. Requires implementing NIST SP 800-171, reporting cyber incidents within 72 hours, using FedRAMP-Moderate cloud for CUI, and flowing the clause down to subs.
DFARS 252.204-7019
Notice of NIST SP 800-171 DoD Assessment Requirements
A contractor must have a current self-assessment score (within the last 3 years) posted in SPRS to be eligible for award. No score, no contract.
DFARS 252.204-7020
NIST SP 800-171 DoD Assessment Requirements
Requires giving DoD access to verify a higher-level (Medium/High) assessment, keeping the score current in SPRS, and ensuring subcontractors also have scores on file.
DFARS 252.204-7021
Contractor Compliance with the CMMC Level Requirement
The clause that makes CMMC itself contractual: the contractor must achieve and maintain the CMMC level named in the contract, affirm compliance, and flow it down to subs at the appropriate level.

Currency note

The 48 CFR CMMC Acquisition rule took effect November 10, 2025, adding DFARS 252.204-7021 to DoD contracts over a four-phase, ~3-year rollout (Phase 1: L1 & L2 self-assessments → Phase 2: L2 C3PAO certifications → Phase 3: L3 → Phase 4: all). So whether 7021 appears in a given solicitation depends on the phase and program — always read the actual contract.

Q A contract contains FAR 52.204-21 but none of the DFARS 7012/7021 clauses, and the work involves no CUI. What does this tell you?
The organization handles FCI only, so the obligation is basic safeguarding (the 15 FAR requirements) → CMMC Level 1. Without 7012/7021 or any CUI, Level 2 is not triggered by this contract.
Q Which single clause makes a specific CMMC level a contractual requirement (not just a self-score)?
DFARS 252.204-7021. 7012 requires implementing NIST SP 800-171 and 7019/7020 deal with posting/verifying the self-assessment score, but 7021 is what obligates the contractor to achieve and maintain the named CMMC level and flow it down.

Module 2 The Applicability Decision Tree

Once you know the clauses and the data, applicability becomes a short series of questions. Work them top to bottom — the first one that fits gives you the answer. The two inputs you bring from your earlier lessons are “Is there FCI?” and “Is there CUI?”

1Does the organization perform (or subcontract on) a federal/DoD contract, or otherwise handle FCI or CUI for one?
NO →CMMC does not apply. Not Applicable  Verify there is truly no FCI/CUI first.
YES ↓Continue to question 2.
2Does the work involve CUI (per the CUI Registry and your FCI/CUI lesson)?
NO (FCI only) →Basic safeguarding under FAR 52.204-21. CMMC Level 1  15 requirements; annual self-assessment + affirmation.
YES ↓Continue to question 3.
3Has DoD designated this as a highest-priority / critical-program effort requiring enhanced protection?
NO →Standard CUI protection. CMMC Level 2  110 NIST SP 800-171 controls; self-assessment or C3PAO per the contract/phase.
YES →Enhanced protection. CMMC Level 3  Adds NIST SP 800-172; government (DIBCAC)-led assessment.
?Can't confirm a clause is present or whether the data is CUI?
EITHER →Don't guess. Needs Review  Flag it, note what you'd verify (the clause, the CUI marking, the data flow), and confirm before deciding.
The one rule to remember

Data drives the level. FCI-only work points to Level 1; the presence of CUI points to Level 2; a DoD-designated critical program points to Level 3. The clauses confirm and formalize what the data already tells you.

Q A contractor stores DoD technical drawings marked CUI//SP-CTI on its file server. No special "critical program" designation applies. What CMMC level is indicated, and why?
Level 2. The presence of CUI (the marked technical drawings) moves the organization past Level 1 to the 110 NIST SP 800-171 controls. Nothing indicates a DoD critical-program designation, so Level 3 is not triggered.
Q During the study you find a contract that may involve CUI, but the markings are ambiguous and no DFARS clause is visible. What's the right call?
Needs Review — not a guess. Document what you'd verify (request the clause list, confirm CUI markings with the contracting officer, map the data flow), then decide once confirmed. Guessing risks both over-scoping and under-scoping.

Module 3 Scope & the Assessment Boundary

Knowing CMMC applies is only half the answer — you also need where. The assessment scope is the set of people, processes, and technology that are evaluated; the assessment boundary is the line you draw around them. Get this wrong in either direction and you pay: over-scoping wastes money protecting things that don't need it, while under-scoping leaves CUI exposed and fails the assessment.

For Level 2, every asset is sorted into one of five categories defined in 32 CFR § 170.19. The category decides how (and whether) the asset is assessed and documented in the System Security Plan (SSP).

CUI Assets
Process, store, or transmit CUI
The systems that actually handle CUI — workstations, servers, apps, and storage where CUI lives or moves.
Assessed against all Level 2 requirements
Security Protection Assets (SPA)
Provide security for the CUI environment
Tools/services that protect the scope — SIEM, firewalls, a managed security provider — even if they never touch CUI themselves.
Assessed against the requirements relevant to what they protect
Contractor Risk Managed Assets (CRMA)
Could access CUI but are managed by policy
Assets capable of access but deliberately not allowed to, managed under your risk-based security policies and procedures.
Not fully assessed — but subject to a limited spot-check
Specialized Assets
GFE, IoT, OT, test equipment, restricted systems
May or may not handle CUI; hard to secure conventionally. Must be documented and risk-managed in the SSP.
Documented in the SSP; managed by risk, limited review
Out-of-Scope Assets
No interaction with CUI or its protections
Things with no CUI access and no security role at all — e.g., a marketing laptop on a separate network.
Not assessed, not documented under CMMC
How this maps to the case study

Assignment 6 uses simpler boundary "zones." Line them up like this: Included = CUI Assets + Security Protection Assets (fully in); Shared = assets that straddle the boundary and need documented limits; Supporting = Contractor Risk Managed / Specialized (in scope but risk-managed, not fully assessed); Excluded = Out-of-Scope (with a documented basis for exclusion).

Q A managed service provider runs the contractor's firewall and SIEM but never sees CUI. In-scope or out? Which category?
In scope — a Security Protection Asset (SPA). Because it provides security protection for the CUI environment, it's assessed against the requirements relevant to the protection it provides, even though it doesn't process CUI itself.
Q The sales team's laptops sit on a separate network, never touch CUI, and provide no security function. How are they treated?
Out-of-Scope. With no CUI interaction and no security-protection role, they aren't assessed or documented under CMMC — though you should be able to show the separation that keeps them out.

Module 4 Flowdown to Subcontractors

CMMC obligations don't stop at the prime contractor. When a prime shares covered information with a subcontractor, the relevant clauses flow down — the sub inherits the obligations for the information it handles. This is how protection follows the data through the entire supply chain.

  • What flows down: DFARS 7012 (via paragraph (m)), 7020 (via (g)), and the 7021 CMMC requirement.
  • At what level: the sub must meet the level appropriate to the information it handles — a sub that receives CUI needs Level 2; a sub that handles only FCI needs Level 1.
  • Who's responsible: the prime is accountable for flowing the clauses down and for confirming subs meet the requirement before sharing covered information.
  • The trap: a sub that handles CUI but only achieved Level 1 (or has no current SPRS score) is a flowdown gap — a common applicability-study finding.
Key idea

Flowdown follows the data, not the org chart.

If you hand CUI to a partner, they must protect it to the same standard you do — regardless of their size or role.

Q A prime holding CUI subcontracts heat-treating to a small shop and sends them CUI technical specs. The shop assumes "we're too small for CMMC." Correct?
No. Because the shop receives CUI, the 7012/7021 obligations flow down and it must meet CMMC Level 2 for that work. Size doesn't exempt it — handling CUI does. The prime must confirm this before sharing the specs.

🧪 Worked Example — Putting It Together

Here's the whole method on a neutral sample company (not FFSI) so you can see the reasoning end to end.

Apex Precision Machining — a small defense subcontractor

Apex holds a subcontract from a prime. The subcontract includes DFARS 252.204-7012, -7019, and -7021. The prime sends Apex engineering drawings marked CUI//SP-CTI. Apex stores them on a CAD workstation and a file server; a managed IT provider runs its firewall/SIEM; the front-office laptops handle only invoices on a separate network; a networked CNC machine receives toolpaths generated from the drawings.
  1. Clauses (Module 1): 7012 + 7021 are present → CUI safeguarding and a CMMC level are contractually required; 7019 means a current SPRS score is needed to stay eligible.
  2. Decision tree (Module 2): There's a DoD subcontract ✔ → CUI is present ✔ (the marked drawings) → no critical-program designation → CMMC Level 2.
  3. Scope & boundary (Module 3): CAD workstation + file server = CUI Assets; managed IT provider = Security Protection Asset; the CNC machine = Specialized Asset (document in SSP); front-office laptops (separate network, no CUI) = Out-of-Scope.
  4. Flowdown (Module 4): If Apex sends any of that CUI to its own vendor (say, a coating shop), 7012/7021 flow down and that vendor must also meet Level 2 for the CUI it receives.
  5. Recommendation: CMMC applies at Level 2; scope is the CAD/file-server/security stack plus the documented CNC; exclude the front office with evidence of separation; verify downstream vendors before sharing CUI.

✅ Recap

  • Clauses are the trigger — FAR 52.204-21 (FCI → L1); DFARS 7012/7019/7020/7021 (CUI → L2, with 7021 making the level contractual).
  • Data drives the level — FCI → Level 1, CUI → Level 2, DoD critical program → Level 3; when unsure, Needs Review.
  • Scope is sorted into five categories — CUI Assets & SPAs are fully assessed; CRMAs & Specialized Assets are risk-managed/documented; Out-of-Scope assets are neither.
  • Flowdown follows the data — subs handling CUI must meet Level 2; the prime is responsible for flowing clauses down and confirming compliance.
